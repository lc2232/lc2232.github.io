# Introduction

When applying for jobs there is one document that often determines whether you reach the next interview stage: the CV (or “resume”, as this course refers to it).

The course was created by Gergely Orosz, author of _The Pragmatic Engineer Newsletter_ and _The Tech Interview Inside-Out_. It focuses on the process and key pitfalls to avoid when applying for software engineering roles, providing practical tools to fine-tune your CV into one that stands out in a crowded market.

This blog post is a summary of the notes I had taken while reading through this course. Hopefully they can be used again by someone (maybe even myself) in the future to highlight some interesting pieces of advice I hadn't considered before.

## Key Takeaways

- Write your CV for **human readers first**, not ATS systems.
- Prioritise **clarity, short sentences, and measurable impact**.
- **Referrals significantly improve your chances** of progressing in the hiring pipeline.
- Only link to **GitHub, LinkedIn, or websites that are maintained**.
- Tailor each application by **matching keywords and themes from the job description**.

# The Human Factor

While ATS (Applicant Tracking Systems) tools are widely used, the course argues that their role is often exaggerated. In practice, they are mainly used to parse information and track candidates rather than automatically rejecting them. Whether this holds true in the age of LLM-driven screening is debatable, but Orosz emphasizes that your CV must be human-readable first. This means using clear formatting, shorter sentences, action verbs, and statistical metrics to quantify your impact.

Before this course, I mistakenly believed a CV’s primary job was to bypass an ATS and then serve as a reference for interviewers. Orosz dispelled this quickly. "Keyword stuffing," which I’ve done in the past, is painfully obvious to hiring managers who scan hundreds of resumes a day.

## Readability

An interesting tool I had not seen before is the [Hemingway Editor](https://hemingwayapp.com) which provides a readability score to the text you input. It also provides insight into "weakeners" which make writing sound hesitant or unconfident, which is the last thing you want to be conveying in a CV.

## Structure

The course provides several templates for use with various ratings as to their effectiveness. These focus on clear structure, making it easier for recruiters to parse and extract key information such as dates, positions and companies.

The best rated templates in the course are the following:
- [Markdown to resume formatter](https://github.com/mikepqr/resume-markdown)
- [Mono Engineers Resume](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1DrAED8s8DWlTgCMNMVnFUxrzn_BgwxSTCd_JyLFIEzw/edit?pli=1&tab=t.0)
- [Pragmatic Engineers resume](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1UBMuHp6cDVfdP508xP7H9wgLc4VMj6KfyjhAWB9k4OY/edit?tab=t.0)

These templates enforce a clean structure and aim to stop the user from cramming too much into the CV and congesting the page. A common pitfall of many CVs.

## Pipeline

The hiring process is essentially a funnel designed to find a single person for a role. Each phase reduces the number of candidates. One of the most effective ways to "fast-track" your way through this pipeline is via a personal recommendation. Orosz suggests this drastically improves your chances, especially if you receive a referral link that bypasses the standard application portal.

### How to get recommendations?

Knowing someone at the company is the easiest route, but you can also leverage LinkedIn to reach out directly to employees or hiring managers. Recruiters are also valuable allies; it’s their job to find great talent, so they are often happy to give you the inside scoop on the process.

## Beyond the Resume

### GitHub/LinkedIn/Website

A lot of software engineers will have a personal website, GitHub and LinkedIn. These are all great to include on your CV, however they are only worth adding if they have been maintained. The course mentions how sometimes linking to an inactive or poorly maintained GitHub/Website can negatively impact your chances with a hiring manager. Therefore if you add a link to your CV, ensure it **works and the content it links to is tidy**.

### Cover Letters

I have read online quite a few Reddit posts ([post #1](https://www.reddit.com/r/careerguidance/comments/1lo04sa/do_we_still_need_to_write_a_cover_letter_for/), [post #2](https://www.reddit.com/r/jobs/comments/xtbaf0/recruiters_how_important_is_a_cover_letter/)) around the topic of whether cover letters are a waste of time or not. While the _reddit verdict_ is mixed, Orosz argues that a well-crafted, concise cover letter never hurts.

From my perspective, cover letters are a useful way to explain your personal narrative. Hiring managers are ultimately trying to understand the person behind the CV. If your CV and cover letter fail to convey that clearly, they likely need to be reworked to better communicate how your experience fits the role.

The course links some good blog posts around writing good software engineer cover letters:
- [Stack Overflow](https://stackoverflow.blog/2016/11/11/developer-cover-letter/)
- [Junior Software Engineer Cover Letter](https://openupthecloud.com/punchy-junior-software-developer-cover-letter/)
- [X Thread](https://x.com/svpino/status/1290970001856397312?s=20)

## Tailoring

There is a lot of talk about people searching for jobs who think of it as a numbers game, and go for quantity over quality. This course dispels such narratives and talks about how you should tailor your experiences to the job description.

A practical tip shared in the course is to highlight the key words in the job description that describe the company culture and the person they're looking to hire. Then use these exact words in your CV and cover letter. Make sure these are different for each job you apply for.

The course suggests using a website such as [Jobscan](https://www.jobscan.co/) to provide metrics on how well you have tailored your CV to fit the description.

# Conclusion

Overall, the course helped me rethink how CVs are evaluated from the hiring side of the process. The biggest takeaway was that clarity and readability matter far more than clever formatting or keyword tricks.

It only took a few hours to complete, but it provided several practical ideas I plan to apply in my future job applications. If you are preparing for software engineering roles, it is a useful resource to review and I highly recommend it.
